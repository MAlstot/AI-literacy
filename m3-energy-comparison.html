M3.3 — The Hidden Cost of a Prompt: AI Energy at a Glance

TRAIL • Module 3: AI and Ethics • Baylor University

Start here: AI's energy use is not one number. It depends on what you ask it to do, which model you use, and the scale of use. The first tab explains the types of AI use; the next three show how task, tool, and volume change the footprint.
Types of AI use
AI tasks fall into two families. The difference explains most of the energy story in the other tabs.
Sorting & classifyingWorking with content that already exists. Lower energy.
Text classification
Sorting text into categories. An email marked spam or not spam; a review flagged positive or negative.
Extractive question answering
Finding an answer inside a document and pulling out the exact words. You ask "what is the refund deadline," and it highlights the line in the policy. It locates, it does not compose.
Masked language modeling
Predicting a missing word from context. The autocomplete that fills in "see you ___ the meeting."
Token classification
Labeling each word in a sentence. Tagging "Baylor" as an organization and "Waco" as a place, or marking names to redact from a document.
Image classification
Labeling a whole image with one category. "Cat or dog," or a scan flagged normal or abnormal.
Object detection
Finding and boxing the things in an image. Your phone drawing squares around faces, or a backup camera spotting a pedestrian.
GeneratingCreating something new. Higher energy, images highest.
Text generation
Writing new text. Drafting an email, a chatbot reply, or finishing a sentence you started.
Summarization
Condensing a long text into a short one. Turning a twelve-page report into a three-sentence recap.
Image captioning
Describing a picture in words. Automatic alt text: "a child flying a red kite on a beach."
Image generation
Creating a brand-new image from a description. Typing "a watercolor mountain cairn at sunrise" and getting a picture. The most energy-intensive task in the study.
Energy differs by family Tasks in the first family work with content that already exists; tasks in the second create new content. In the measurements in the next tabs, tasks in the first family use less energy per use than tasks in the second, and image generation uses the most.

Image generation, compared with the other tasks At 2.907 kWh per 1,000 images, image generation uses about 46 times the next-highest task (image captioning) and about 1,450 times text classification. A single image, on the least efficient model measured, uses about half a smartphone charge. In carbon terms, 1,000 images on the heaviest model measured equal roughly the emissions of driving 4 miles in an average gasoline car.
What the measurements show Across the tasks measured, energy per 1,000 uses ranges from 0.002 kWh (text classification) to 2.907 kWh (image generation). Sorting and classifying tasks fall at the low end, text generation and summarization in the middle, and image tasks at the high end.
Data note: Means across representative open models on standardized hardware (NVIDIA A100, Oregon grid). Image generation varies widely with image size, up to 11.49 kWh per 1,000 on the least efficient model. These are open models measured directly, not proprietary systems such as ChatGPT. Training and scale
One-time training footprints for four similar-sized models, and why the grid matters as much as the model

Energy and emissions can diverge BLOOM used more energy than OPT (433 vs. 324 MWh) but emitted less (25 vs. 70 tonnes), and about 20 times less than GPT-3. The four models trained on grids of differing carbon intensity, from 57 gCO₂eq/kWh for BLOOM to 429 for GPT-3.
Training vs. inference Training is a one-time cost; inference recurs with each query. Per query, inference is small, but volume is large: ChatGPT drew about 1.7 billion visits in October 2023. Estimates place inference at 60 to 90 percent of operational ML compute. In one deployment study, about 75 percent of the energy went to keeping the model loaded in memory rather than answering queries.
Data note: Training figures from Luccioni, Viguier & Ligozat (2023), Table 4; GPT-3 also reported in Patterson et al. (2021). Energy and carbon are shown separately because carbon depends on the grid, not just the computation. GPT-3's training, about 502 tonnes (552 with data-center overhead), is roughly the lifetime emissions of five average cars.
